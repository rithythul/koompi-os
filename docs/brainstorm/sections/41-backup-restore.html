Backup & Restore

Durability & Threat Model

§30 snapshots protect you from yourself — a bad update, a deleted folder. They live on the same disk. They do nothing against fire, theft, or a confiscated laptop. A Lifetime OS that concentrates fifty years of one person's memory on a single SSD owes a hard answer to the question the Vision audit flagged as critical: what happens when the disk is gone — or taken? Owning your data means owning its durability and its destruction. Backups stay yours, encrypted with keys we never hold; and a stolen machine in a politically sensitive context cannot become a single point of catastrophic exposure.

Who holds the key? You. Only you.

Apple escrows your FileVault recovery key to iCloud. Microsoft uploads your BitLocker key to your account by default. Convenient — and a key a court can subpoena. KOOMPI does neither. The key is generated on-device and never transmitted.

This is the one warning we will not soften: if you lose this recovery key, your backup is mathematically gone. We cannot recover it. There is no "forgot password" because there is no one to ask.

Duress unlock · refused ✕ security theater
An earlier draft of this page demoed a duress PIN unsealing a decoy hidden volume, silently severing the backup link, and firing a coercion heartbeat over Selendra. It is cut, honestly: a second LUKS keyslot and a decoy volume are visible to any competent forensic examination — fake deniability is worse than none, because it teaches you to lean on a story that evaporates exactly when it matters, and a discovered decoy can convert a search into an obstruction charge. KOOMPI does not ship protections that only work if the adversary is careless.

We hold no keys, no copies, no cloud Sovereign

Apple escrows FileVault keys to iCloud; Microsoft uploads BitLocker keys to your account. Both are a subpoena away. KOOMPI's backups encrypt on-device with keys that never leave it — which means we genuinely cannot recover your data, and we say so in amber rather than hiding behind a silent cloud upload. Sovereignty has a cost, and we make you the one who pays it knowingly.

A stolen laptop is not a catastrophe

Concentrating fifty years of memory locally is only defensible if the disk being gone or taken is survivable. FDE turns a stolen drive into ciphertext; remote and dead-man wipes discard the key in under a second. The 3-2-1 map guarantees the data still exists elsewhere — for you, and no one else.

Theater refused — no duress decoys

An earlier draft shipped a duress PIN that opened a decoy life, with "the absence of evidence" as its protection. Cut: a second keyslot and a decoy volume are evidence — visible to competent forensics — so the deniability fails exactly when it matters, and a discovered decoy can make things legally worse. The limit we documented then is the reason it is gone now: no software survives you confessing it exists. The honest answers are boring and real — FDE at rest, lockout throttling, key-discard wipes, and three copies of your data that travel without the laptop.

Backups proven, not assumed

The detail every consumer backup tool skips: an untested backup is a guess. koompi-restore mounts the archive read-only each week, checksums a random sample against the Merkle root anchored on Selendra, and tells you the day a drive silently dies — not the day you reach for it after a disaster. Lifetime durability means the restore is verified for decades, not hoped for.
