Backup & Restore

Durability & Threat Model

§30 snapshots protect you from yourself — a bad update, a deleted folder. They live on the same disk. They do nothing against fire, theft, or a confiscated laptop. A Lifetime OS that concentrates fifty years of one person's memory on a single SSD owes a hard answer to the question the Vision audit flagged as critical: what happens when the disk is gone — or taken? Owning your data means owning its durability and its destruction. Backups stay yours, encrypted with keys we never hold; and a stolen machine in a politically sensitive context cannot become a single point of catastrophic exposure.

Apple escrows your FileVault recovery key to iCloud. Microsoft uploads your BitLocker key to your account by default. Convenient — and a key a court can subpoena. KOOMPI does neither. The key is generated on-device and never transmitted.

Duress unlock — for when "just open it" isn't a request
A border agent, a raid, a kidnapping for a wallet seed. A lock you can be forced to open is not a lock. So KOOMPI lets you set a second PIN that opens a different, plausible, complete life — and there is no setting, badge, or indicator anywhere that admits the first life exists. That absence is the whole feature.

What actually happened (only you can know)
The duress PIN unsealed a decoy hidden volume. Your real home — fifty years of memory, your true Selendra keys, your real backup credentials — sits in unallocated-looking space the decoy cannot see and cannot prove is there. Entering duress also silently severs the live backup link, so an attacker can't follow it to your off-site copy.
Optional: duress unlock fires a signed Selendra heartbeat to your guardians — "opened under coercion" — that looks like nothing on this screen.
Engineering honesty: plausible deniability is only as strong as your discipline. If the decoy's clock is frozen at install or its backups never run, a careful adversary notices. KOOMPI keeps the decoy alive — it ages, syncs, and accumulates plausible noise on its own — but no software can protect you from admitting it exists. We document that limit rather than pretend it away.

We hold no keys, no copies, no cloud Sovereign

Apple escrows FileVault keys to iCloud; Microsoft uploads BitLocker keys to your account. Both are a subpoena away. KOOMPI's backups encrypt on-device with keys that never leave it — which means we genuinely cannot recover your data, and we say so in amber rather than hiding behind a silent cloud upload. Sovereignty has a cost, and we make you the one who pays it knowingly.

A stolen laptop is not a catastrophe

Concentrating fifty years of memory locally is only defensible if the disk being gone or taken is survivable. FDE turns a stolen drive into ciphertext; remote and dead-man wipes discard the key in under a second; and duress mode means even forced cooperation surrenders a decoy life, not your real one. The 3-2-1 map guarantees the data still exists elsewhere — for you, and no one else.

Decoy mode that admits nothing

A "hidden vault" toggle that shows up in settings is theatre — it proves a second volume exists the moment anyone looks. KOOMPI's duress unlock leaves no badge, no setting, no keyslot, no metadata an adversary can point to. The real protection is the absence of evidence. We also document where deniability ends: no software survives you confessing it exists.

Backups proven, not assumed

The detail every consumer backup tool skips: an untested backup is a guess. koompi-restore mounts the archive read-only each week, checksums a random sample against the Merkle root anchored on Selendra, and tells you the day a drive silently dies — not the day you reach for it after a disaster. Lifetime durability means the restore is verified for decades, not hoped for.
