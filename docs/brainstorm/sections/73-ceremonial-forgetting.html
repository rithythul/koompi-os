Ceremonial Forgetting

Futures Privacy

The Memory Palace promises a tombstone: "The OS will never recall this. No copy exists." The Memory Lifecycle promises the opposite: "raw episodic records are immortal." Both promises are right — they were just one verb short. A 50-year OS holds grief, trauma, and the legal right to erasure alongside the duty to never lose a life by accident. The resolution: forgetting is three verbs, not one — and the strongest of them is a ceremony, never a button.

Two promises in conflict — both already shipped in this document
MEMORY PALACE · §17
"Every memory has a delete that leaves a tombstone, never a copy. No copy exists."
MEMORY LIFECYCLE · §59 · INVARIANT A
"Raw episodic records are immortal. Forgetting is a ranking property, never a delete."
Resolution — Invariant A′: episodic records are immortal by default. The OS may never delete; decay and pruning still touch only the index. But the owner holds one additional power the OS does not: cryptographic destruction — granted only through ceremony, never as a side effect, never on impulse.
Three verbs of forgetting
Suppress recoverable

Rank pinned to zero. The Ebbinghaus machinery the OS already runs, pushed to its floor: never surfaced, never volunteered, never woven into a briefing. The record stays in @data — the right cue, deliberately given, still rebuilds it.

for: the ex-employer, the abandoned startup, the noise
Seal time-locked

Re-encrypted under a key you do not hold today: released to your future self after a chosen date, or to your executor under the Death Protocol. Gone now — not forever. The OS cannot read it, cite it, or infer from it while sealed.

for: grief — the year she died, sealed until 2036
Destroy irreversible

Cryptographic erasure. The per-object DEK is shredded; the ciphertext in @data becomes noise. The next sleep cycle runs a forget pass: every derived trace — vectors, graph nodes, summaries — is purged and re-consolidated without it. A tombstone records that, never what.

for: trauma · the legal right to erasure

The OS speaks once, in calm register
"I will carry this for four more days, in case you change your mind. After that, I will not remember it — and I will not remember why there is a gap. You can stop this at any time before Thursday."
Tombstone after the act: "A memory spanning Mar–Sep 2031 was destroyed by its owner on 14 Jun 2033, after ceremony." That a thing was forgotten is remembered. What it was, is not.
The asymmetry is the safety
The OS may suppress (ranking — it does this nightly). Only you may seal. Only you may destroy, and only through the ceremony. No daemon, no update, no cloud policy, no other person holds the destroy verb — and neither does a 2 a.m. version of you.
owner-only
One verb was three KOOMPI

"Delete" conflated silence, distance, and erasure. Suppress quiets a memory, Seal banishes it to your future self, Destroy ends it cryptographically. The Memory Palace tombstone and the immortal episodic store were never enemies — they were Destroy and Suppress, missing their names.

Grief, trauma, and the law

Grief usually wants Seal — the year you cannot look at yet, returned when you are ready. Trauma and the legal right to erasure want Destroy — and get it, with dignity. An OS that holds 50 years of a life owes its owner all three, not a euphemism for one.

Never by accident, never by impulse

Forgetting is never a side effect: no cascade, no retention policy, no storage pressure can trigger it. And destruction is never an impulse: the waiting period is the OS protecting the future you from the worst night of your life. Revocable until the final hour, then absolute.
